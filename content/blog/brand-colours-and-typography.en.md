Before a potential customer reads a single word on your website or brochure, they have already formed an impression. That impression is built almost entirely from two things: colour and type. Most business owners think of these as decorative choices — a matter of personal taste. In reality, they are functional tools that communicate values, set expectations, and quietly tell people whether to trust you.

## Why Colour Does More Work Than You Think

Colour is processed faster than language. When someone lands on your website or picks up your business card, their brain is already making associations before conscious thought kicks in. A deep navy paired with warm off-white reads very differently from a bright orange on a white background — even if the words on both are identical.

This is not about rigid rules like "blue means trust" or "green means nature." Those generalisations fall apart quickly in practice. What matters more is *consistency* and *contrast*. A carefully limited palette — typically two to three colours — signals that someone has made deliberate decisions. That deliberateness itself communicates professionalism. When colours are chosen haphazardly or shift across touchpoints (website, social media, printed materials), the cumulative effect is a subtle but real sense of unreliability.

Contrast also has a practical dimension: accessibility. Sufficient contrast between text and background is not just good design — it ensures that your content is actually readable for people with visual impairments, and it is increasingly expected as a baseline standard.

## Typography Sets the Tone Before the Text Does

Typography is the part of visual identity that most businesses underestimate. A typeface carries personality. A condensed geometric sans-serif feels modern and efficient. A classic serif with generous spacing feels established and considered. A rounded, friendly sans-serif feels approachable and informal. None of these is inherently better — but each creates a different expectation in the reader's mind.

The most common mistake is treating font choice as a branding afterthought, then using too many of them. Two typefaces — one for headings, one for body text — is almost always enough. More than that and the visual system starts to feel noisy and unresolved. The relationship between the two faces matters as well: they should complement each other without competing. A bold display font paired with a highly readable body face is a reliable foundation.

Size, weight, line spacing, and letter spacing all contribute too. Generous line spacing makes long text feel less daunting. Consistent heading hierarchy helps readers navigate. These are small decisions with a real cumulative impact on how long someone stays engaged with your content.

## A Concrete Example: The Same Business, Two Different Impressions

Consider a small accountancy firm. Version one uses a clean, dark-green palette, a traditional serif for headings, and a neutral sans-serif for body copy. The overall impression: reliable, experienced, detail-oriented. Version two of the same firm uses a bright coral palette, an informal rounded sans-serif throughout, and inconsistent spacing. The services offered are identical — but the second version will likely face more hesitation from clients who are about to hand over sensitive financial information.

Neither palette nor typeface is objectively wrong. But only one is *aligned* with what the business actually needs to communicate. That alignment — between what you do, what you value, and what your visual identity signals — is the goal.

## Consistency Is the Real Deliverable

Getting the initial choices right is only half the job. The other half is applying them consistently. A brand colour that appears in five slightly different shades across your materials, or a heading font that gets swapped out whenever someone makes a new document, erodes the coherence that makes visual identity work in the first place.

This is why it helps to document your choices — even simply, even early. A short brand style guide that records your exact colour values (HEX, RGB, CMYK), your chosen typefaces, and basic rules for how they are used gives everyone working on your brand — internally or externally — a shared reference point. It turns good decisions into repeatable habits.

Colour and typography will never be the whole story of a brand. But they are the first chapter, and they are read faster than any headline you write. Taking them seriously is one of the most straightforward ways to make your business look and feel like the professional operation it already is.
