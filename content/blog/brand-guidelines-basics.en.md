A business can have a beautiful logo and still look like a different company every time someone encounters it online, in print, or in a presentation. That gap — between having a brand and actually living it consistently — is exactly what brand guidelines are designed to close. Whether you are a team of three or thirty, a clear set of design standards is one of the most practical investments you can make in how your business presents itself to the world.

## What Brand Guidelines Actually Contain

Brand guidelines (sometimes called a style guide or corporate design manual) are a documented set of rules that define how your brand looks, sounds, and feels across all applications. The scope can vary, but a solid set of guidelines typically covers:

- **Logo usage** — approved versions, minimum sizes, clear space requirements, and what *not* to do (stretching, recolouring, placing on clashing backgrounds).
- **Colour palette** — primary and secondary colours with exact values for print (CMYK), screen (RGB/HEX), and sometimes specialist formats like Pantone.
- **Typography** — which typefaces to use, in what weights, at what sizes, and how headings relate to body text.
- **Imagery and photography style** — the mood, subject matter, and editing approach that fits the brand, so photos feel cohesive rather than random.
- **Tone of voice** — how the brand writes: formal or conversational, first or third person, words it avoids.
- **Layout principles** — spacing, grid systems, and how elements are arranged on a page or screen.

Some organisations also include icon styles, illustration guidelines, and rules for co-branding with partners. The level of detail depends on how many people will be producing brand materials and across how many channels.

## How Consistency Breaks Down (and How Guidelines Prevent It)

Without written standards, consistency erodes quietly. A new team member picks a font that looks "close enough." A supplier creates a brochure using a slightly different shade of blue. A social media post goes out with the logo cropped awkwardly. None of these feels catastrophic in isolation, but over time they add up to a brand that looks uncertain of itself — and audiences notice, even if they cannot articulate why.

Guidelines give everyone a shared reference point. Instead of relying on memory or asking the original designer every time, a colleague or external partner can open the document and find the answer. This is especially important as a business grows: the more people involved in producing communications, the more valuable a single source of truth becomes.

It is worth noting that guidelines are not about rigidity for its own sake. A well-written style guide explains the *reasoning* behind each rule, which makes it easier for people to apply them sensibly in situations the document did not anticipate.

## A Concrete Example: A Regional Service Business Expanding Across Channels

Imagine a family-run engineering firm in Tirol that has operated for years with a logo, a website, and a few printed brochures — all created at different times by different people. The colours are slightly off between the website and the print materials. The font on the brochure is not the one used on the site. The logo appears in three different proportions depending on where you look.

When the firm decides to expand into new markets and launch a proper digital presence, these inconsistencies become a real problem. Prospective clients in Munich or Zurich are forming first impressions from multiple touchpoints simultaneously: a Google search result, a LinkedIn profile, a downloaded PDF. If each one looks subtly different, trust is harder to build.

By developing a clear set of brand guidelines at this point — defining exact colours, locking down typography, establishing logo rules — the firm creates a foundation that all future materials can be built on. A new website, trade fair stand, or email signature can be produced by anyone, anywhere, and still look unmistakably like the same company.

## Keeping Guidelines Alive and Useful

A brand guideline document is only valuable if people actually use it. The most effective approach is to make it easy to access — a shared digital file rather than a printed booklet that lives in a drawer — and to revisit it whenever the brand evolves. Guidelines should be a living reference, updated when new channels are added or when the visual identity is refreshed.

It also helps to pair the document with a small library of ready-made templates: presentation decks, letterheads, social media post formats. When the right tools are already built to spec, the path of least resistance becomes the path of consistency.

Brand guidelines are not a luxury reserved for large corporations. For any growing business that wants to look credible and coherent — across a website, a proposal, a trade fair, and everything in between — they are a practical, working tool.
